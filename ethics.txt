Ethics Questions – CS106A Assignment 6: Bajillion Search Engine
================================================================

Question 1:
One of the goals of a search engine is to "democratize" access to
information – to make it possible for anyone to find what they need.
In what ways could the design of a search engine undermine that goal,
even if unintentionally?

Answer:
A search engine can undermine democratized access in several ways.
First, the ranking algorithm determines which results users see first,
and if ranking favors popular or well-established sources, smaller or
marginalized voices may be effectively silenced even though they are
technically indexed. This creates a feedback loop: popular pages get
more clicks, which reinforces their ranking, while newer or minority
perspectives are buried.

Second, the way content is indexed matters. Our simple search engine
treats all documents equally, but real-world engines make choices about
what content to crawl and how often to re-index it. If a search engine
disproportionately crawls content in English or from wealthy nations,
it creates an access gap for users seeking information in other
languages or from underrepresented communities.

Third, the query interface itself can be a barrier. Users who are more
experienced with technology may construct better queries and thus get
better results, while less tech-savvy users may struggle. Autocomplete
and suggestion features, while helpful, can also steer users toward
popular interpretations of a query and away from niche but valid ones.

Finally, search engines can be manipulated through techniques like SEO
(Search Engine Optimization). Entities with more resources can invest
in SEO to ensure their content ranks higher, effectively buying
visibility at the expense of those who lack such resources. This
commercialization of search results works against the democratic ideal.


Question 2:
If you were building a search engine that would be used by millions of
people, what is one ethical consideration you would want to think about
carefully as you designed it?

Answer:
I would think carefully about user privacy and data collection. A
search engine used by millions of people has access to an extraordinary
amount of personal information – not just names and emails, but the
queries themselves, which reveal what people are curious about, worried
about, or struggling with. Search queries can expose health concerns,
political beliefs, financial difficulties, and deeply personal matters.

This data, if stored and analyzed, can be used to build detailed
profiles of individuals. While personalization can improve search
quality, it also creates risks: data breaches could expose sensitive
queries, and aggregated data could be sold to advertisers or handed
over to governments. Users searching for sensitive topics – such as
information about addiction, domestic abuse, or political dissent –
may face real-world consequences if their queries are linked back to
their identities.

As a designer, I would want to minimize data retention (collecting only
what is necessary and deleting it promptly), provide transparency about
what data is collected and how it is used, offer users meaningful
control over their data (including the ability to delete their search
history), and resist pressure to share user data with third parties
without clear legal obligations. I would also consider offering an
anonymous search mode where queries are not linked to user accounts at
all. The guiding principle should be that the search engine exists to
serve its users, not to extract value from them.
